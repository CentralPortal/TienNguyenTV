1. Information to Ask From Each Family

▼

The goal is to gather enough information to understand their needs and locate them for future visits, without making them feel interrogated.

Basic Identification & Location:

  • Full Name of the Head of Household
  • Village/Hamlet Name: The specific small community they live in.
  • District/Commune Name: The larger administrative area.
  • General Directions to Home: Simple landmarks or descriptions of the route (e.g., "past the big rock," "turn left at the stream," "up the hill from the market"). This is crucial for remote areas.
  • Best Time to Visit: When are family members most likely to be home?

Family Composition:

  • Total Number of Family Members living in the household.
  • Number of Children (especially those of school age or younger).
  • Number of Elderly (e.g., over 60 or 65).
  • Any Family Members with Special Needs (e.g., disabilities, chronic illness).

Living Conditions:

  • Type of Home: Is it a cement house, a wooden house, or a makeshift shelter (tree branches, tarps, aggregate tin sheets)? This gives a quick visual of their situation.
  • Access to Clean Water: Do they have a reliable source of clean drinking water? If so, what is it (well, river, rainwater collection)?
  • Access to Light/Electricity: Do they have electricity or another source of light at night?

Livelihood & Needs:

  • Primary Occupation(s): What do they do for a living (e.g., farming, collecting manure, herding, daily labor)?
  • Main Challenges: What are the biggest difficulties they face in their daily lives or work? (This helps gauge their poverty level qualitatively without asking for exact income).
  • Most Urgent Need (Beyond Food): If you could help with one thing right now, what would it be (e.g., shelter repair, medicine, school supplies, clean water system)?

2. Comfortable Questions for Assistants to Query Families

▼

Train your assistants to be empathetic, patient, and respectful. Frame questions as a way to understand and help, not to judge.

Start with Gratitude & Warmth:

  • "Hello, thank you so much for coming today. We are very happy to be able to share these food supplies with you."
  • "My name is [Assistant's Name], and I'm helping Mr. Tien today. He truly cares about supporting families in the mountain regions."

Transition to Information Gathering (Explain the "Why"):

  • "To help Mr. Tien understand the needs of families better, and so he can potentially visit and offer more support in the future, would you be willing to share a little bit about your family and where you live?"
  • "This information will only be used by Mr. Tien and his team to help more families, and it will be kept private."

Gentle Questioning Examples:

  • Name & Location: "Could you please tell us your name and the name of your village? It helps us know where to find you if Mr. Tien comes to visit." "If Mr. Tien wanted to visit your home, how would you describe the easiest way to get there? Are there any big trees or rivers nearby?"
  • Family: "How many people live in your household?" "Do you have children who go to school, or elderly parents living with you?" "Are there any family members who need special care due to health?"
  • Home: "We see many different kinds of homes here. Could you tell us a little about your home? Is it made of wood, or perhaps tarps and branches?" "Do you have easy access to clean water for drinking and cooking?" "Is it easy to get light in your home, especially at night?"
  • Work & Needs: "What kind of work do you usually do to support your family?" "What are some of the biggest challenges you face in your daily life or with your work?" "Beyond the food today, what is the most urgent need for your family right now? Is it perhaps help with your home, or medicine, or something for the children?"

3. Encouraging People to Sign Up

▼

Hesitancy is natural due to past experiences or shyness. Focus on building trust and explaining the positive impact.

  • Build Trust First: The act of giving food is the first step. Show genuine care and respect throughout the interaction.
  • Clearly Explain the Purpose: "Mr. Tien's YouTube channel shows the real lives of families in need. When people see these videos, it helps them understand the difficulties and encourages them to donate so we can bring even more help, not just to your family but to others in the community." "By sharing your story, you're not just helping yourself, you're helping us reach more people who want to make a difference for our community."
  • Emphasize Choice and Privacy: "It's completely your choice. There's no pressure at all." "If you agree, Mr. Tien would visit your home, talk with you, and share your story respectfully. We would never share anything you don't want us to." "You can always change your mind later if you sign up today."
  • Highlight Success Stories (if possible): "Many families who have shared their stories with Mr. Tien have received significant help – some have even gotten new homes or regular support for their children's education. You can see their stories on our website."
  • The Info Card (Crucial for Hesitant Families): This is your most important tool for those who are not ready to commit on the spot. It provides a low-pressure way for them to connect later.

4. List of Tasks for Your Assistants

▼

Before the Event:

  • Briefing & Training: Gather all assistants. Explain the event's purpose (food distribution, information gathering, sign-up encouragement).
  • Review Information Sheet: Go over the form they will use to collect family data. Ensure they understand each field.
  • Practice Questions: Role-play the "comfortable questioning" techniques. Emphasize empathy, patience, and active listening.
  • Explain YouTube Channel: Ensure they can clearly articulate the purpose and benefits of being featured on the channel.
  • Distribute Info Cards: Give each assistant a stack of the small info index cards.
  • Cultural Sensitivity: Remind them to be respectful of local customs and traditions.

During the Event:

  • Warm Welcome: Greet each family warmly as they arrive with their ticket.
  • Food Distribution: Efficiently distribute one food package per family ticket.
  • Engage & Build Rapport: Have a brief, friendly conversation with each family. Thank them for coming.
  • Information Collection: Politely explain the purpose of collecting information (for future help). Use the comfortable questioning techniques to fill out the information sheet/form accurately for each family. Ensure they understand the information is confidential.
  • Introduce YouTube Channel & Sign-up: After collecting basic information, gently introduce the idea of being featured on the YouTube channel. Explain how sharing their story helps bring more support. Ask if they are willing to sign up. If yes, mark it on the form.
  • For Hesitant Families: If a family is hesitant, reassure them that it's okay. Say: "We understand if you're not ready today, and that's perfectly fine. Here is a small card with Mr. Tien's name, website, and phone number. You can take it with you." "On the website, you can see videos of other families who have received help. If you ever feel comfortable and want to connect later, please use the number on this card." Hand them the info index card.
  • Thank You & Farewell: Thank each family again for their time and cooperation.

After the Event:

  • Collect Forms: Gather all completed information sheets/forms from assistants.
  • Debrief: Ask assistants about any challenges, common concerns, or notable interactions.
